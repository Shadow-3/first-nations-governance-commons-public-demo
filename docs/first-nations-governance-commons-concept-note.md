# First Nations Governance Commons

Date: 2026-06-04  
Status: v0.1 concept note  
Initial developer/custodian: Barayamal / Dean Foley  
Working model: Wikipedia-style civic commons, adapted for First Nations governance

## One-Line Summary

First Nations Governance Commons is a Barayamal-initiated, First Nations-controlled, Decidim-powered civic infrastructure platform for community mandate, participatory budgeting, consent, agreement tracking, service accountability, and evidence-based advocacy.

## The Problem

Many consultation and engagement processes collect First Nations input but do not clearly show:

- Who authorised the process.
- Who had decision-making rights.
- How offline voices were included.
- What changed because of community input.
- Who is responsible for action.
- Whether governments, funders, companies, or service providers followed through.

This creates consultation fatigue and weak accountability.

## The Proposed Solution

Build a reusable Decidim-based platform and open governance commons that helps First Nations communities run legitimate decision processes and produce clear evidence of mandate.

The platform should support:

- Community proposals.
- Meetings and yarning circles.
- Surveys and voting/support.
- Participatory budgeting.
- Free, Prior and Informed Consent-style processes.
- Youth and Elders spaces.
- Agreement and commitment tracking.
- Service scorecards.
- Evidence pack exports.
- Public, member-only, and restricted governance spaces.

## The Core Product

The core product is not the website. The core product is the **Mandate Record**.

Each major process should record:

- What was asked.
- Who authorised the process.
- Who could participate.
- What information was provided.
- How online and offline input was gathered.
- What the community said.
- What decision was made.
- What conditions apply.
- Who must act.
- What changed.
- What remains unresolved.

## Why Decidim

Decidim is an open-source participatory democracy framework designed for assemblies, participatory processes, proposals, meetings, surveys, debates, voting, participatory budgeting, newsletters, authorisations, and accountability tracking.

This makes it a strong foundation for First Nations civic participation, provided it is adapted with cultural governance, data sovereignty, offline participation, and community authority controls.

## Wikipedia-Style Commons, With Boundaries

The useful part of the Wikipedia model is shared public knowledge, open contribution, transparent history, community stewardship, and reusable infrastructure.

For this project:

- Public templates, guides, training material, and code can be shared.
- Communities can adapt the model for local use.
- Local decision rules, member data, cultural material, and restricted records stay under community control.
- Partners can respond, but they cannot control community processes.

Working rule:

**Open-source the infrastructure. Do not open-source community authority.**

## First Pilot

Recommended first pilot:

**2026 Community Priorities and Budget**

Pilot components:

- Public explainer page.
- Community survey.
- Proposal board.
- Offline yarning circles.
- Online meeting.
- Voting or support phase.
- Final priorities page.
- Accountability tracker.
- Mandate Record.
- Evidence pack export.
